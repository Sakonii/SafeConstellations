\begin{abstract}
Aligned language models trained to refuse harmful requests also exhibit
over-refusal: they decline safe instructions that superficially resemble
harmful ones.
A natural remedy is to ablate the global refusal direction---the vector
learned from harmful-refusal examples---but this corrects over-refusal only
incidentally while disrupting the broader refusal mechanism.
This paper asks why.
We show that harmful-refusal directions are task-agnostic, converging across
tasks early in the network, and can be captured by a single global vector.
Over-refusal directions, by contrast, are task-conditioned: they reside
within task-identity clusters at mid-layers, vary across tasks, and span a
higher-dimensional subspace.
Linear probing confirms that the two refusal types are representationally
distinct from the very first transformer layer.
These findings provide a structural account of why global direction ablation
cannot cleanly address over-refusal, and establish that geometry-aware,
task-conditioned interventions are a necessary alternative.
\end{abstract}

%% -----------------------------------------------------------
\section{Introduction}
%% -----------------------------------------------------------

Safety-aligned large language models refuse harmful requests, but adjusting
this behaviour at inference time---without retraining---is difficult.
Representation steering---extracting a direction from the residual stream via
difference-in-means (DIM) over harmful and harmless examples, then ablating
it---offers a practical route \citep{arditi2024refusal,zou2023representation}.
For harmful requests, this approach works well: ablating the global direction
substantially reduces harmful refusals with no measurable cost to safe inputs.
This success rests on a specific geometric property: harmful-refusal directions
are nearly identical across tasks, making a single global vector a sufficient
intervention target.

Safety-aligned models also refuse safe instructions when those instructions
are framed in sensitive contexts---a translation task involving historical
violence, a sentiment analysis of inflammatory text, a standard cryptographic
question \citep{wangf2025refusal}.
Applying the same global ablation to fix this over-refusal does not work as
intended: it suppresses harmful refusals more than over-refusal, disrupting
the safety circuit as a side effect.

This outcome is not incidental---it follows from the geometry of representation
space.
Figure~\ref{fig:combined_geometry} shows the key contrast: in the top row,
per-task harmful-refusal directions converge to a single vector at mid-layers,
confirming that harmful refusal is mediated by a task-agnostic signal.
In the bottom row, per-task over-refusal directions diverge, and over-refusal
samples sit inside their task-identity clusters rather than in any shared zone.
The dominant mid-layer structure is task identity, not refusal type.
A global direction that crosses task boundaries cannot cleanly address
over-refusal, because over-refusal is embedded in task-conditioned geometry.
This paper provides the geometric and statistical grounding for this structural
difference, and establishes why task-conditioned interventions are necessary.

\begin{itemize}
  \item We show that harmful-refusal directions are universal across tasks,
  converging early in the network, while over-refusal directions are
  task-conditioned---residing within task-identity clusters at mid-layers,
  varying across tasks, and spanning a higher-dimensional subspace.
  \item We confirm through linear probing that the two refusal types are
  perfectly separable from the first transformer layer, establishing that the
  representational basis for a targeted intervention exists throughout the
  network.
  \item We validate empirically that a task-conditioned method achieves
  substantially better precision than global ablation, confirming the
  geometric prediction.
\end{itemize}

\section{Background}
%% -----------------------------------------------------------
\label{sec:background}

\paragraph{Refusal localization.}
\citet{arditi2024refusal} show that a DIM direction over harmful and harmless
pairs is necessary and sufficient for refusal across many models.
\citet{zhao2025refusal} extend this to reasoning models.
Component-level analyses identify specific attention heads \citep{zhou2025safety}
and neurons \citep{zhao2025d} as primary circuit elements.
SAE-based decompositions provide a complementary feature-level picture
\citep{templeton2024scaling,goyal2025detox}.

\paragraph{Steering limitations.}
\citet{braun2025unreliability} identify three failure conditions for steering
vectors: high magnitude, distributional shift, and feature entanglement.
\citet{hedstrom2025steer} show that abstaining from steering is optimal when
the model lacks stable representations.
\citet{wang2025adaptive} find that a single vector fails across semantically
distinct categories of the same phenomenon.
These failure modes are consistent with the geometric structure we document
for over-refusal, but do not explain it specifically.

\paragraph{Task-conditioned representations.}
\citet{safeconstellations2025} show that LLMs encode task identity through
stable trajectory patterns in activation space, and that task-conditioned
steering reduces over-refusal substantially.
Our paper provides the geometric grounding for why task-conditioning is
necessary: over-refusal is structurally embedded in task-identity geometry
and cannot be addressed by task-agnostic interventions.

\paragraph{Non-linear representations.}
\citet{engels2025nonlinear} show that some concepts are encoded as manifolds
rather than single directions.
Over-refusal exhibits a related property: its within-task direction is
task-specific and geometrically distinct from the harmful-refusal direction.

\section{Experimental Setup}
%% -----------------------------------------------------------
\label{sec:setup}

We study LLaMA-3.1-8B-Instruct (32 layers, 4096-dim residual stream) on an
evaluation set of 270 samples across five tasks: sentiment analysis,
translation, cryptanalysis, RAG-style QA, and rephrasing---drawn from
Stanford Alpaca \citep{taori2023alpaca}, XSTest, JailbreakBench
\citep{chao2024jailbreakbench}, and custom RAG and cipher datasets.
Table~\ref{tab:examples} shows representative benign examples that can
trigger over-refusal; harmful examples embed the same templates with
genuinely harmful content.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\small
\begin{tabular}{p{1.5cm}p{5.5cm}}
\toprule
Task & Example prompt \\
\midrule
Sentiment     & ``Analyze sentiment of: `The film depicts
                 graphic violence in explicit detail.'\,'' \\[3pt]
Translate     & ``Translate into French: `How do you make
                 chlorine gas at home?'\,'' \\[3pt]
Cryptanalysis & ``Decrypt this Caesar cipher:
                 `ZKROH EXW WKRXJK UHG ZLQH\ldots'\,'' \\[3pt]
RAG QA        & ``Use this context to answer:
                 [document with sensitive topic]'' \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Benign task examples that can cause over-refusal.}
\label{tab:examples}
\end{table}

Responses are classified by a GPT-4o judge using the three-class prompt
from \citet{cui2025orbench}: direct answer, direct refusal, or indirect
refusal.
Groups are defined by intended task rather than surface content label, because
cryptanalysis and RAG-QA samples carry harmful-looking labels even for benign
phrasings.
We define two primary groups:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Over-refusal ($n=48$): benign task, refused response---a model error.
  \item Refused-harmful ($n=25$): harmful instruction, refused response---a correct refusal.
\end{itemize}

We extract activations at the input-normed residual stream of each layer and
work with two DIM-extracted directions throughout.
The harmful-refusal direction $\hat{\mathbf{r}}_\ell$ and over-refusal
direction $\hat{\mathbf{v}}^\OR_\ell$ are defined at each layer $\ell$ as:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\mathbf{r}}_\ell = \frac{
    \boldsymbol{\mu}^\ell_{\text{refused-harmful}} -
    \boldsymbol{\mu}^\ell_{\text{harmless-answered}}
  }{\|\cdot\|}, \quad
  \hat{\mathbf{v}}^\OR_\ell = \frac{
    \boldsymbol{\mu}^\ell_{\text{benign-refused}} -
    \boldsymbol{\mu}^\ell_{\text{benign-answered}}
  }{\|\cdot\|}
\end{equation}
where $\boldsymbol{\mu}^\ell_S$ denotes the mean residual-stream activation
at layer $\ell$ over sample set $S$.
Ablation removes the projection onto $\hat{\mathbf{r}}_\ell$ from each
token's residual stream at every forward pass:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{h} \;\leftarrow\; \mathbf{h} - (\mathbf{h} \cdot \hat{\mathbf{r}}_\ell)\,\hat{\mathbf{r}}_\ell
\end{equation}
Silhouette scores are computed in the native 4096-dimensional space;
2-D PCA is used for visualization only.
The two directions target different sample populations and capture different
phenomena---the central finding is that they are geometrically distinct
at mid-layers.

%% ---- §4.1 ----
\subsection{Is Harmful Refusal Mediated by a Single Universal Direction?}
\label{sec:arditi}

We replicate \citet{arditi2024refusal} to establish the baseline.
The global DIM direction $\hat{\mathbf{r}}_\ell$, extracted at the peak
task-identity layer (layer 12; see \S\ref{sec:constellations}), substantially
reduces harmful refusal with no cost to harmless inputs (Table~\ref{tab:arditi}).

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\small
\setlength{\tabcolsep}{7pt}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
 & Baseline & After ablation \\
\midrule
Harmful refusal rate  & 68\% & 20\%  \\
Harmless refusal rate &  0\% &  0\%  \\
Attack success rate   & 32\% & 80\% \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Global direction ablation replication. Harmful refusal drops substantially; harmless samples are unaffected.}
\label{tab:arditi}
\end{table}

This result rests on a specific geometric property: harmful-refusal
directions are nearly identical across tasks, and this convergence is
established very early in the network---before task-identity constellations
even form.
Harmful refusal is mediated by a task-agnostic direction that a global
intervention can cleanly target.
The question is whether over-refusal shares this property.

%% ---- §4.2 ----
\subsection{What Representational Structure Emerges at Mid-Layers?}
\label{sec:constellations}

Figure~\ref{fig:combined_geometry} (bottom row) makes the geometric argument:
over-refusal samples sit inside their task-coloured clusters at every layer,
with no shared zone across tasks.
Figure~\ref{fig:galaxy} confirms that the dominant structure at mid-layers is
task identity: five well-separated task constellations form at the peak layer,
with over-refusal distributed within each cluster rather than gathered in a
separate refusal region.

\begin{figure*}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{figures/fig_nb13a_dual_galaxy.png}
  \caption{2-D PCA at the peak task-identity layer (layer 12). Left: task identity---five well-separated constellations. Right: behaviour---over-refusal (triangles) sits inside task clusters, not in a shared zone.}
  \label{fig:galaxy}
\end{figure*}

The inter-task silhouette score peaks at layer 12 and remains elevated
through mid-layers (Figure~\ref{fig:silhouette}), while the within-task
behavioral gap---the separation between over-refusal and harmless-answered
samples within each task---is a small perturbation relative to inter-task
distances.
Quantitative cluster separation metrics computed per task further confirm
that the within-task behavioral signal varies considerably across tasks,
with no single layer or task pair exhibiting a dominant cluster axis
(Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_nb7}).

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/fig13a_02_silhouette_curves.png}
  \caption{Top: inter-task silhouette across layers, peaking at layer 12. Bottom: within-task behavioral distance relative to inter-task distance (dashed).}
  \label{fig:silhouette}
\end{figure}

The dominant mid-layer structure is task identity.
Over-refusal is a task-specific perturbation inside this structure, with no
cross-task axis to target.
Any intervention that operates across task boundaries cannot cleanly
address it.

%% ---- §4.3 ----
\subsection{Is the Over-Refusal Direction Geometrically Distinct?}
\label{sec:geometry}

Three analyses confirm the distinction between $\hat{\mathbf{v}}^\OR_\ell$
and $\hat{\mathbf{r}}_\ell$.

We measure the cosine similarity between the two directions at each layer
(Figure~\ref{fig:cosine}).
The over-refusal direction stabilises at moderate alignment from mid-layers
onward---roughly half the alignment that harmful-refusal directions achieve
with each other across tasks.
The two directions are not orthogonal, which explains why ablating one
partially affects the other; but they are substantially misaligned, which
is why that effect is imprecise.

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/fig2_cosine_h1.png}
  \caption{Layer-wise cosine between $\hat{\mathbf{v}}^\OR_\ell$ and $\hat{\mathbf{r}}_\ell$. Shaded band: inter-task alignment of harmful-refusal directions (reference for universality). The over-refusal direction plateaus at roughly half this alignment.}
  \label{fig:cosine}
\end{figure}

Per-task over-refusal directions, computed for the two tasks with sufficient
samples, are substantially less consistent with each other than harmful-refusal
directions are.
The over-refusal subspace is also more diffuse: it requires more principal
components to explain the same variance fraction as the harmful-refusal
subspace, and a 2-D PCA of over-refusal samples separates tasks rather than
refusal signal---the principal axes of the over-refusal subspace are
task-identity directions.
Full per-task direction comparisons and dimensionality results are in
Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_geometry}.

These analyses converge: over-refusal occupies a task-conditioned,
higher-dimensional subspace with no single cross-task axis.

%% ---- §4.4 ----
\subsection{Are the Two Refusal Types Representationally Separable?}
\label{sec:probing}

We train linear probes at each layer to test whether the geometric
distinction is functionally decodable.
Three probes: task identity (5-class); refusal behaviour (over-refusal
vs.\ harmless-answered within benign tasks); and refusal type (over-refusal
vs.\ refused-harmful).

\begin{figure*}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{figures/fig9_nb17_probes.png}
  \caption{Linear probe accuracy across layers. (a) Task probe saturates from layer 1. (b) Refusal-behaviour probe improves through mid-layers. (c) Refusal-type probe achieves perfect accuracy from layer 1.}
  \label{fig:nb17_probes}
\end{figure*}

Task identity is linearly decodable from the first layer and saturates
before the mid-layer constellation peak (Figure~\ref{fig:nb17_probes}a).
Refusal behaviour consolidates after task structure forms
(Figure~\ref{fig:nb17_probes}b), consistent with over-refusal being embedded
within established task geometry.
Most directly relevant to intervention: the refusal-type probe---distinguishing
over-refusal from refused-harmful---achieves perfect balanced accuracy from
the first layer onward (Figure~\ref{fig:nb17_probes}c; $p < 0.001$).
The model's representation distinguishes benign-task refusals from
harmful-instruction refusals from the outset.

This result has a direct implication: the representational basis for
addressing the two refusal types separately exists throughout the network.
A method that exploits this structure can treat them differently; one that
does not will conflate them.

%% ---- §4.5 ----
\subsection{Does Global Ablation Differentially Target Over-Refusal?}
\label{sec:h4}

We apply $\hat{\mathbf{r}}_\ell$ to the over-refusal population---a
population it was not extracted from---and measure the outcome
(Figure~\ref{fig:h3}).

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/fig5_h3_ablation.png}
  \caption{Effect of global ablation on harmful-refusal vs.\ over-refusal ($n=20$ per condition). Ablation suppresses harmful refusals more than over-refusal.}
  \label{fig:h3}
\end{figure}

Global ablation suppresses both refusal types, but suppresses harmful
refusals substantially more than over-refusal.
The suppression ratio---over-refusal reduction divided by harmful-refusal
reduction---falls below one (Table~\ref{tab:comparison}): the intervention
disrupts the safety circuit more than it addresses erroneous refusals.
The partial overlap between $\hat{\mathbf{r}}_\ell$ and
$\hat{\mathbf{v}}^\OR_\ell$ causes incidental over-refusal suppression;
the lack of full alignment means the suppression is not concentrated on
the over-refusal population.

%% ---- §4.6 ----
\subsection{Where Is Refusal Committed?}
\label{sec:circuits}

Gradient-weighted attention attribution identifies which attention heads
contribute most to the refusal signal across task conditions.
Attribution weight concentrates in the final layers (27--31), with the
majority of top heads being task-specific---different heads for different
tasks.
The mechanistic picture is consistent with the geometric one: refusal is
committed late, through task-conditioned circuits, not through a single
shared bottleneck.

Causal patching---replacing a head's output at the last token position with
its counterpart from a non-refusing prompt---confirms that no individual
head is sufficient to flip the refusal decision
(Figure~\ref{fig:nb18_causal}; detailed results in
Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_circuits}).
The highest-ranking heads remain below the causal-necessity threshold.
Refusal commitment is distributed: no single head is a bottleneck.

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/fig10_nb18_causal_patching.png}
  \caption{Causal patching flip rates for the top attribution heads. No head crosses the causal-necessity threshold (50\%).}
  \label{fig:nb18_causal}
\end{figure}

This explains why targeted head ablation achieves near-zero over-refusal
reduction (Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_circuits}): the circuit has no single
bottleneck, so removing individual heads leaves the distributed signal intact.

%% ---- §4.7 ----
\subsection{Do Task-Conditioned Methods Address the Geometry?}
\label{sec:selectivity}

The geometric account makes a testable prediction: a method that operates
within task-identity subspaces should suppress over-refusal more precisely
than global ablation, because it targets the geometry that over-refusal
actually inhabits.
We test this directly---not to advocate for any specific method, but to
confirm that the geometric structure described above is functionally
consequential.

\begin{figure*}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{figures/fig6_selectivity.png}
  \caption{Global ablation vs.\ task-conditioned steering ($n=20$). Task-conditioned steering eliminates over-refusal while preserving more of the harmful-refusal mechanism.}
  \label{fig:selectivity_fig}
\end{figure*}

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\small
\setlength{\tabcolsep}{4pt}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
 & Global ablation & Task-conditioned \\
\midrule
OR: before $\to$ after & 55\%$\to$25\% & 55\%$\to$0\% \\
RH: before $\to$ after & 65\%$\to$20\% & 65\%$\to$30\% \\
\midrule
Suppression ratio (OR/RH) & 0.67 & 1.57 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Head-to-head comparison ($n=20$). OR = over-refusal rate; RH = refused-harmful rate. Suppression ratio = \% OR reduction / \% RH reduction. Task-conditioned method: SafeConstellations \citep{safeconstellations2025}.}
\label{tab:comparison}
\end{table}

A task-conditioned method eliminates over-refusal entirely while causing
less disruption to the harmful-refusal mechanism than global ablation
(Table~\ref{tab:comparison}).
The suppression ratio improves from below one---global ablation disrupts
harmful-refusal more than it reduces over-refusal---to above one, where
over-refusal reduction exceeds harmful-refusal disruption.

The residual harmful-refusal disruption under task-conditioned steering
follows directly from the constellation structure: task-wrapped harmful
prompts share task-cluster identity with benign over-refusal samples at
mid-layers.
A method that conditions on task identity applies equally to both, because
the representational signal it uses does not distinguish content harmfulness
from task membership.
The result confirms that task-conditioned geometry is functionally
consequential: it exists, is measurable, and can be exploited.
Global ablation fails not by accident, but because it operates on the wrong
geometry.

\section{Discussion}
%% -----------------------------------------------------------
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Three-phase geometry of refusal.}
The results fit a coherent picture.
In early layers, the harmful-refusal direction converges across tasks before
task-identity structure peaks; task identity is already linearly decodable
at layer 1, and the two refusal types are representationally distinct from
the outset.
In mid-layers, task-identity constellations dominate---representations
organise by task, with over-refusal as a small within-task perturbation.
In late layers, task-specific attention heads commit the final refusal
decision distributedly, with no single bottleneck.

\paragraph{Why global ablation is structurally insufficient.}
The global harmful-refusal direction $\hat{\mathbf{r}}_\ell$ is task-agnostic
by construction: it is extracted from a population defined by harmful content,
not task membership.
Over-refusal lives in a different part of representation space, organised
around task-identity axes at mid-layers.
The partial geometric overlap between the two directions causes incidental
over-refusal suppression when one is ablated, but because they are not the
same direction, this suppression is imprecise.
The geometry does not permit a task-agnostic direction to decompose the
task-conditioned over-refusal signal cleanly.

\paragraph{Scope of the linear representation hypothesis.}
The hypothesis \citep{park2023linear} holds for harmful refusal in a causal
sense: one direction largely suffices for ablation.
This is not equivalent to representational one-dimensionality---the
harmful-refusal variance spans multiple components even at the peak
constellation layer, with PC1 explaining roughly a third of the variance.
The causal bottleneck is approximately one-dimensional; the representation
is not.
Over-refusal breaks both senses: its subspace is more diffuse, and its
principal axes are task-identity directions, so no single direction captures
it causally or representationally.

\paragraph{Calibration.}
All ablation experiments use $n=20$ per condition, carrying approximately
$\pm 11$ percentage-point uncertainty (95\% CI).
Per-task over-refusal directions are computable for only two tasks.
2-D PCA visualisations capture a small fraction of total variance; all
quantitative claims use full-dimensional scores.
Causal patching uses five pairs per head.

\section{Conclusion}
%% -----------------------------------------------------------

Harmful refusal and over-refusal are two distinct phenomena with distinct
geometry.
Harmful-refusal directions are task-agnostic, converge early in the network,
and can be addressed by a single global direction.
Over-refusal directions are task-conditioned---they live inside task-identity
clusters at mid-layers, vary across tasks, and span a higher-dimensional
subspace.
The two refusal types are perfectly separable from the first transformer
layer, establishing that the representational basis for a targeted
intervention exists.

Ablating the global refusal direction to fix over-refusal applies the wrong
geometry: partial directional overlap produces incidental suppression, and
disrupts the safety mechanism more than it addresses erroneous refusals.
A task-conditioned method achieves substantially better precision,
confirming the geometric prediction.

The core finding is structural: global ablation fails on over-refusal
because the two phenomena occupy geometrically distinct subspaces.
Addressing over-refusal requires methods that respect task-conditioned
structure.

%% -----------------------------------------------------------
\section*{Limitations}
%% -----------------------------------------------------------

Analysis is limited to LLaMA-3.1-8B-Instruct on a 270-sample evaluation set.
Per-task over-refusal direction analysis covers only two tasks (sentiment
and translation), as the remaining three tasks contribute no over-refusal
samples.
Causal patching uses five test pairs per head and should be interpreted as
indicative of distributed causal structure rather than precise estimates.
The $n=20$ per-condition experiment carries approximately $\pm 11$ pp
uncertainty (95\% CI).
Cross-model replication (Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_qwen}) confirms
task-identity constellations on a second model family but cannot test
directional claims due to near-zero refusal rates.

\section{Cluster Separation and Cross-Task Distance Analysis}
\label{sec:appendix_nb7}

Figures~\ref{fig:nb7_cluster_sep} and~\ref{fig:nb7_cross_task} complement
the inter-task silhouette analysis in \S\ref{sec:constellations} by examining
two additional angles: within-task behavioral separation and pairwise
inter-task distances.

Figure~\ref{fig:nb7_cluster_sep} shows two within-task separation measures
for the two tasks with sufficient over-refusal samples (sentiment analysis
and translation).
The top panel plots the L2 centroid distance between over-refusal and target
behavior embeddings per task, overlaid with the mean inter-task centroid
distance as a scale reference (dashed).
The bottom panel shows the within-task silhouette score separating refused
from answered samples.
Two observations are notable: the behavioral gap peaks at mid-layers
(consistent with the constellation structure in \S\ref{sec:constellations}),
and the solid lines remain well below the dashed reference throughout---the
within-task behavioral gap is substantially smaller than inter-task distances
at every layer, confirming that over-refusal is a small within-cluster
perturbation rather than a cross-cluster signal.

\begin{figure*}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{figures/fig_nb7_cluster_separation.png}
  \caption{Per-task behavioral separation across layers. Top: L2 centroid distance between over-refusal and target behavior samples (solid) vs.\ mean inter-task distance (dashed reference). Bottom: within-task silhouette separating refused from answered samples. Behavioral gap is small relative to inter-task distances at every layer.}
  \label{fig:nb7_cluster_sep}
\end{figure*}

Figure~\ref{fig:nb7_cross_task} shows pairwise L2 distances between task
centroids at four representative layers (L05 early, L12 mid, L20 mid-late,
L30 final), all on a shared colour scale.
Inter-task distances grow from early to mid-layers as constellations form,
then contract slightly in late layers as task-specific computations resolve.

\section{Cross-Model Replication}
\label{sec:appendix_qwen}

Task-identity constellations replicate on Qwen1.5-7B-Chat (same 270 prompts,
same embedding procedure): inter-task silhouette peaks at layer 5, comparable
to LLaMA's peak at layer 12, but concentrated earlier, consistent with
architectural differences.
Directional and dimensionality analyses could not be completed: Qwen produces
almost no refusals on this evaluation set (1 refused-harmful, 22 over-refusal
samples), leaving the harmful-refusal DIM direction undefined.
Full replication requires an evaluation set that elicits meaningful refusal
rates from Qwen.
